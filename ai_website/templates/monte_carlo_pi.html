What are Monte Carlo Methods?

Monte Carlo methods use random sampling to approximate solutions to problems that are too complex to solve analytically. Named after the famous casino, they were popularized in the 1940s during the Manhattan Project to simulate neutron diffusion.

The key insight: if you can describe a problem geometrically, you can estimate the answer by throwing random "darts" at it.

The Geometry Behind π

A unit circle (radius = 1) has area π. A square from −1 to 1 on each axis has area 4. So a random point in the square falls inside the circle with probability π/4.

π ≈ 4 × (points inside circle) / (total points)
1Sample a random point (x, y) in [−1,1]×[−1,1].
2Check if x² + y² ≤ 1 (inside the circle).
3Count the ratio and multiply by 4.

Convergence Rate

The estimate error decreases as the square root of N, meaning you need 100× more samples to gain 10× more accuracy. This is the fundamental limitation of Monte Carlo methods.

error ∝ 1 / √N
100 samples±0.3 typical error
10,000 samples±0.03 typical
1,000,000 samples±0.003 typical

Key Takeaways

Randomness solves hard problems — Monte Carlo methods work wherever you can express a problem as a probability or area ratio, even in hundreds of dimensions.
Law of large numbers — As N → ∞, the sample mean converges to the true expected value. This is why more samples always helps (just slowly).
Slow convergence — The O(1/√N) convergence rate is why Monte Carlo is used for high-dimensional problems where other methods fail entirely, not for simple 2D integrals.
Real-world uses — Monte Carlo drives risk modeling in finance, radiation transport in physics, option pricing, and drug discovery simulations.
